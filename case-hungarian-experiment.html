The Hungarian Experiment

How far can a healthy brain go? One subject, ten weeks, an intensive cognitive protocol — and instrumented measurement on both ends to find out what actually moved.

Subject
Male, 25–34 · healthy baseline
Window
~10 weeks · baseline → exit
Instruments
qEEG · 12-domain cognitive battery
Oversight
Clinical neurofeedback therapist
The protocol

Train four domains. Measure all twelve.

The stated goal: improved focus, and increased knowledge acquisition and retention — pressure-tested against a concrete challenge: acquiring Hungarian, one of Europe's most difficult languages, using memory-palace techniques built during the program.

The protocol specialized in four targets — working memory, inhibitory control, spatial memory, and deductive reasoning — through intensive cognitive training layered with meditation progressions, deep work, nutrition and nootropic design, gamified cognitive drills, and progressive relaxation. Everything else was left untrained — deliberately. A twelve-domain battery on both ends shows what moved, and what it cost.

Curious what your twelve look like?

Three things happened. The training landed where it was aimed: working memory, inhibitory control, and deductive reasoning climbed — deductive from Poor to Excellent. Transfer appeared where it wasn't: planning and episodic memory rose untrained. And the ledger has a cost column: verbal short-term memory and two visual-processing domains slipped — the price of ten weeks allocated hard toward executive and memory work.

No protocol moves everything. A measured one shows exactly what moved, what held, and what it cost — the difference between a program and a promise.

The electrophysiology

A second instrument, pointed at the same claim.

Cognitive tests measure output. qEEG measures the machinery underneath — electrical activity source-localized to specific brain regions, scored against a normative database. Taken before and after, under a clinical neurofeedback therapist's supervision.

Prefrontal / medial frontal — BA 11
Planning · reasoning · decision-making
Post-training: relative power shifted toward normative range; hi-beta (a distractibility signature) reduced across frontal and temporal regions.
Temporal lobe — BA 21
Memory · language processing
A pronounced theta deviation localized here — the same region loaded by ten weeks of memory-palace training and language acquisition.

These maps matter for one reason: convergence. The regions that changed are the regions the training targeted: focus and decision-making sharpening in the prefrontal cortex, memory consolidating in the temporal lobe. Two instruments that don't share a failure mode, telling one story.

That's the standard: not one flattering metric, but agreement across methods. It's also the point — the same machinery that let one man master an impossible language is the machinery running your hardest days. It can be measured. It can be trained.

The outcome

Did the training survive contact with life?

The pressure test was Hungarian — one of the hardest languages a native English speaker can attempt. He learned it. The memory palaces built in weeks five and six became the vehicle, and the language stuck.

Then the results compounded beyond the experiment: Peter leveraged his publicly documented journey into co-founding a real estate collective. That's the pattern this practice is built on — trained capability converts into career leverage, and the returns don't stop at the exit assessment.

The bridge

Peter trained these against Hungarian grammar. You'd train them against whatever your arena throws at you — same machinery, same measurement, different stakes. This experiment is the origin: the n=1 that seeded the methodology, refined in the field since with high performers.
